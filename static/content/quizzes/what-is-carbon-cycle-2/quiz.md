## Question 1

Which process releases carbon dioxide from both plants and animals?

- [x] Respiration
  > You got it right! Respiration is the process where living organisms break down glucose using oxygen, releasing energy and carbon dioxide.

- [ ] Combustion
  > Nice try! Combustion releases carbon dioxide, but it happens when fuels or organic matter are burned, not in normal plant or animal metabolism.

- [ ] Photosynthesis
  > Nice try! Photosynthesis actually removes carbon dioxide from the air and converts it into glucose, so it doesn't release CO2.

- [ ] Decomposition
  > Nice try! Decomposition releases carbon back into the soil and air after organisms die, not during normal life processes.

---

## Question 2

Why do plants release carbon dioxide at night?

- [x] They respire like animals at night.
  > You got it right! At night, photosynthesis stops, so plants release carbon dioxide through respiration, just like animals.

- [ ] They produce carbon dioxide only when it rains.
  > Nice try! Rain does not trigger carbon dioxide production. Plants release CO2 at night regardless of rainfall.

- [ ] They store oxygen and later convert it to carbon dioxide.
  > Nice try! Plants do not convert stored oxygen into carbon dioxide. Respiration releases CO2 directly from glucose.

- [ ] They release carbon because sunlight warms their leaves.
  > Nice try! Sunlight affects photosynthesis, but it doesn't cause plants to release carbon dioxide at night.

---

## Question 3

A farmer notices fallen leaves decomposing in the soil. Which statement best describes the carbon cycle process happening here?

- [ ] Leaves convert carbon into oxygen before being decomposed.
  > Nice try! Plants do not convert carbon into oxygen after death; decomposition releases carbon as CO2 and into soil.

- [x] Decomposers break down leaves and release carbon into soil and air.
  > You got it right! Fungi and bacteria decompose dead leaves, returning carbon to the soil and atmosphere for reuse in the ecosystem.

- [ ] Carbon is permanently stored in fallen leaves until it is burned or disturbed.
  > Nice try! Carbon in leaves is not stored forever; decomposition naturally returns it to the environment.

- [ ] Carbon is released only if the leaves are broken down completely over many years.
  > Nice try! While decomposition can take time, carbon is gradually released during the process, not only at the very end.

## Question 4

Which scenario demonstrates carbon cycling between organisms and their environment?

- [ ] Plants absorb sunlight to grow taller and stronger.
  > Nice try! Plants use sunlight for energy, but this does not illustrate carbon moving through living organisms and the environment.

- [ ] Rivers carry nutrients away from the land into oceans.
  > Nice try! Rivers move nutrients, but this does not show carbon cycling through organisms specifically.

- [ ] Animals drink water and excrete waste, leaving no carbon.
  > Nice try! Animals do excrete waste containing carbon, so this statement is incorrect.

- [x] Decomposers break down dead organisms and release carbon.
  > You got it right! Decomposers recycle carbon from dead plants and animals back into the soil and air, completing part of the carbon cycle.

---

## Question 5

Which statement best shows the role of carbon in maintaining ecosystem energy flow?

- [ ] Carbon disappears when animals exhale.
  > Nice try! Exhalation releases carbon dioxide, but carbon is not lost from the ecosystem—it is recycled.

- [ ] Carbon is only stored in dead plants and animals.
  > Nice try! Carbon is stored temporarily in dead organisms, but it also cycles through living things and the environment.

- [x] Carbon is constantly recycled through the food web and environment, providing energy.
  > You got it right! Carbon moves continuously through plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment, supporting energy flow in ecosystems.

- [ ] Carbon moves through the air, soil, water, and living things but does not directly support energy in ecosystems.
  > Nice try! Carbon cycling does directly support energy flow by moving through the food web and allowing organisms to grow and survive.
