## Question 1

What percentage of carbon dioxide do oceans absorb from the atmosphere?

- [ ] About 5%
  > Nice try! The oceans actually absorb much more than 5%. They're like giant sponges for carbon dioxide, soaking up about a quarter of what we produce!

- [x] About 25%
  > Excellent! You got it right! Our oceans absorb about 25% (one quarter) of all the carbon dioxide humans produce. That's a huge help for our planet!

- [ ] About 50%
  > Nice try! While oceans are amazing carbon absorbers, they soak up about 25%, not half. Forests and soil absorb carbon too!

- [ ] About 75%
  > Nice try! That would be incredible, but oceans absorb about 25% of our CO2. The rest stays in the atmosphere or gets absorbed by land plants and soil.

> Hint: Think about fractions - it's about one quarter!

## Question 2

What are phytoplankton?

- [ ] Large fish that live in the deep ocean
  > Nice try! Phytoplankton are actually super tiny - so small you need a microscope to see them! They're not fish at all, but tiny plant-like organisms.

- [x] Tiny ocean plants that absorb carbon dioxide
  > Amazing! Phytoplankton are microscopic plant-like organisms that float in the ocean and use carbon dioxide just like trees and plants on land do!

- [ ] Coral reefs found in warm waters
  > Nice try! Coral reefs are different from phytoplankton. Phytoplankton are tiny floating plant-like organisms that absorb CO2, while corals are animals that build reef structures.

- [ ] Underwater volcanoes
  > Nice try! Phytoplankton have nothing to do with volcanoes. They're actually tiny ocean plants that help absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere!

> Hint: The word 'phyto' comes from the Greek word for plant!

## Question 3

Why are oceans sometimes called 'carbon sponges'?

- [ ] Because they release carbon dioxide into the air
  > Nice try! It's actually the opposite - oceans soak up CO2 from the air, not release it. That's why they're called 'sponges'!

- [x] Because they absorb and store carbon dioxide
  > You're a superstar! Just like a sponge soaks up water, our oceans soak up carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and store it. Great thinking!

- [ ] Because sea sponges live in the ocean
  > Nice try! While sea sponges do live in the ocean, that's not why we call oceans 'carbon sponges.' It's because oceans absorb CO2 like a sponge absorbs water!

- [ ] Because the ocean floor is soft and spongy
  > Nice try! The term 'carbon sponge' refers to how oceans absorb carbon dioxide, not what the ocean floor feels like!

## Question 4

What happens when oceans absorb too much carbon dioxide?

- [ ] The water becomes cleaner and healthier
  > Nice try! Actually, too much CO2 makes the ocean more acidic, which can be harmful to sea creatures. It's like adding too much lemon juice to water!

- [x] The oceans become more acidic
  > That's right! When oceans absorb too much CO2, a chemical reaction makes the water more acidic. This is called ocean acidification and it's harmful to marine life.

- [ ] The water turns a different color
  > Nice try! While too much CO2 does change the ocean's chemistry, it doesn't change its color. The real problem is that it makes the water more acidic.

- [ ] The ocean gets warmer
  > Nice try! While climate change does warm the oceans, absorbing CO2 specifically makes the water more acidic, not warmer. These are two different problems!

> Hint: Think about what happens when you add something sour to water...

## Question 5

Which marine creatures are most harmed by ocean acidification?

- [ ] Jellyfish
  > Nice try! Jellyfish don't have shells or hard skeletons made of calcium, so they're less affected by acidification. Coral reefs and shellfish are much more vulnerable!

- [x] Coral reefs and shellfish
  > Correct! Coral reefs and shellfish need calcium carbonate to build their shells and skeletons. Acidic water makes it harder for them to do this, which is why they're in danger.

- [ ] Whales and dolphins
  > Nice try! Whales and dolphins breathe air and don't have shells, so acidification doesn't directly hurt them as much. Coral and shellfish are more at risk!

- [ ] Seaweed and kelp
  > Nice try! Seaweed and kelp can actually benefit from more CO2 since they use it for photosynthesis. Creatures with shells, like coral, are more affected.

> Hint: Think about creatures that need to build hard shells or skeletons...

## Question 6

How do phytoplankton help fight climate change?

- [ ] They produce pollution that blocks sunlight
  > Nice try! Phytoplankton don't produce pollution. They actually help by absorbing carbon dioxide through photosynthesis, just like trees do!

- [ ] They eat plastic in the ocean
  > Nice try! Phytoplankton don't eat plastic. They help fight climate change by absorbing carbon dioxide and producing oxygen through photosynthesis!

- [x] They absorb carbon dioxide and produce oxygen
  > Fantastic! Phytoplankton are tiny heroes! They use photosynthesis to absorb CO2 and release oxygen, producing about 50% of the oxygen we breathe!

- [ ] They cool down the water temperature
  > Nice try! Phytoplankton don't directly cool the water. Their superpower is absorbing carbon dioxide through photosynthesis, which helps reduce greenhouse gases!

> Hint: Phytoplankton do something similar to what trees and plants do on land!

## Question 7

What process do both land plants and ocean phytoplankton use to absorb CO2?

- [ ] Respiration
  > Nice try! Respiration is actually the opposite - it's when living things use oxygen and release CO2. Photosynthesis is what absorbs CO2!

- [ ] Evaporation
  > Nice try! Evaporation is when water turns into vapor. The process that absorbs CO2 is called photosynthesis!

- [x] Photosynthesis
  > You nailed it! Photosynthesis is the amazing process where plants and phytoplankton use sunlight to convert CO2 and water into food and oxygen!

- [ ] Condensation
  > Nice try! Condensation is when water vapor turns back into liquid water. Plants and phytoplankton use photosynthesis to absorb CO2!

> Hint: This word starts with 'photo' which means light in Greek!

## Question 8

If oceans absorb 25% of our CO2, where does most of the rest go?

- [ ] It disappears into space
  > Nice try! CO2 doesn't escape into space. Most of it stays in our atmosphere, acting like a blanket that traps heat. That's why we need to reduce emissions!

- [x] It stays in the atmosphere
  > Correct! After oceans and forests absorb their share, about 45% of CO2 stays in the atmosphere. That's why reducing emissions is so important!

- [ ] It sinks underground automatically
  > Nice try! CO2 doesn't naturally sink underground. While some is absorbed by soil and plants, most extra CO2 stays in the atmosphere.

- [ ] Animals breathe it all in
  > Nice try! Animals do breathe in some oxygen, but they actually breathe OUT carbon dioxide! Most excess CO2 stays in our atmosphere.

> Hint: Think about where greenhouse gases build up...

## Question 9

What would happen to marine life if we stopped adding so much CO2 to the atmosphere?

- [ ] Nothing would change
  > Nice try! Reducing CO2 would actually help a lot! The oceans would become less acidic over time, which would help coral reefs and shellfish recover.

- [ ] All fish would disappear
  > Nice try! Actually, reducing CO2 is good news for fish! Less acidic oceans mean healthier coral reefs, which provide homes for many fish species.

- [x] Oceans would become less acidic and marine life would be healthier
  > Exactly right! When we reduce CO2 emissions, oceans can slowly recover and become less acidic. This gives coral reefs and sea creatures a better chance to thrive!

- [ ] The ocean would freeze over
  > Nice try! Reducing CO2 won't freeze the oceans. It would actually help keep ocean temperatures stable and reduce harmful acidification!

> Hint: Less CO2 means less acid in the ocean water...

## Question 10

Why are healthy oceans important for fighting climate change?

- [ ] They reflect sunlight back into space
  > Nice try! While ice reflects sunlight, oceans are actually dark and absorb heat. Oceans fight climate change by absorbing CO2, not reflecting light!

- [x] They absorb CO2 and support phytoplankton that produce oxygen
  > Outstanding! Healthy oceans are climate heroes! They absorb about 25% of our CO2, and the phytoplankton living in them produce about half of Earth's oxygen!

- [ ] They block wind and storms
  > Nice try! Oceans don't block storms - they can actually create them! Oceans help fight climate change by absorbing CO2 and supporting oxygen-producing phytoplankton.

- [ ] They create rain clouds that cool the planet
  > Nice try! While oceans do help create rain through evaporation, their main role in fighting climate change is absorbing CO2 and supporting phytoplankton!

> Hint: Think about the two important things you learned oceans do with carbon dioxide and oxygen!
