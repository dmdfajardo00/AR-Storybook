## Question 1

Why are forests called the 'lungs of the Earth'?

- [ ] Because they are shaped like lungs
  > Nice try! While forests can look like they have branching patterns, they're called 'lungs' because of what they do, not how they look.

- [x] Because they absorb CO2 and release oxygen, like breathing
  > Excellent thinking! Just like our lungs take in air and exchange gases, forests breathe in carbon dioxide and breathe out oxygen for all of us to use.

- [ ] Because animals use them to breathe
  > Nice try! Animals do breathe in forests, but forests are called 'lungs' because the trees themselves do the breathing work for our whole planet.

- [ ] Because forests make breathing sounds in the wind
  > Nice try! While wind through trees does make sounds, forests are called 'lungs' because they exchange gases just like real lungs do.

> Hint: Think about what lungs do - they take in one gas and release another!

  > Nice try! While trees are amazing carbon absorbers, 200 pounds per year is a bit too high. The actual amount is closer to 48 pounds.

- [ ] About 1 pound
  > Nice try! Trees are actually superstar carbon absorbers! A large tree can absorb about 48 pounds of CO2 each year - way more than just 1 pound.

## Question 3

What do trees release into the air that we need to breathe?

- [ ] Carbon dioxide
  > Nice try! Trees actually absorb carbon dioxide. They release something else that humans and animals need to breathe.

- [ ] Nitrogen
  > Nice try! While nitrogen is in the air, trees are famous for releasing something else that we breathe in every day.

- [x] Oxygen
  > That's right! Trees are amazing oxygen factories. When they do photosynthesis, they release fresh oxygen that all animals, including humans, need to survive.

- [ ] Water vapor only
  > Nice try! Trees do release some water vapor, but they also release something much more important - the oxygen we breathe!

> Hint: It's the gas you breathe in right now!

## Question 4

What happens to carbon when a tree stores it?

- [ ] It floats away into space
  > Nice try! Carbon doesn't escape to space. Trees are much smarter - they keep the carbon right here on Earth.

- [x] It becomes part of the tree's trunk, branches, and leaves
  > Fantastic! Trees are like carbon storage tanks. They use the carbon from CO2 to build their wood, bark, leaves, and roots. The carbon stays locked up as long as the tree lives!

- [ ] It immediately returns to the atmosphere
  > Nice try! If that happened, trees wouldn't help us at all! Trees actually hold onto carbon for a very long time.

- [ ] It turns into water
  > Nice try! Carbon can't turn into water. Instead, trees use carbon as a building block to grow bigger and stronger.

> Hint: Think about what trees are made of!

## Question 5

Why is planting new trees important for fighting climate change?

- [ ] Trees make good shade for picnics
  > Nice try! While tree shade is nice, the main reason for planting trees is much bigger - it's about helping the whole planet!

- [x] More trees mean more carbon dioxide is removed from the atmosphere
  > Brilliant! Every new tree is like adding another helper to clean our air. More trees = more CO2 absorbed = a healthier planet for everyone.

- [ ] Trees attract more rain clouds
  > Nice try! While forests can affect local weather, the main climate benefit of planting trees is their ability to absorb carbon dioxide.

- [ ] Planting trees is just for decoration
  > Nice try! Trees are beautiful, but they're also powerful climate heroes that absorb carbon dioxide and help keep our planet cool.

> Hint: Remember what trees 'breathe in' from the air!

## Question 6

What is deforestation?

- [ ] Planting new forests
  > Nice try! That's actually the opposite - planting trees is called reforestation or afforestation. Deforestation means losing trees.

- [x] Cutting down or destroying forests
  > Correct! Deforestation means removing forests, usually for farming, building, or lumber. This is harmful because we lose all those trees that were absorbing CO2.

- [ ] A type of forest animal
  > Nice try! Deforestation isn't an animal - it's actually a word for the destruction of forests, which hurts many animals.

- [ ] Forests growing bigger
  > Nice try! Deforestation is actually the opposite - it means forests getting smaller because trees are being cut down or destroyed.

> Hint: The prefix 'de-' often means 'removing' or 'undoing' something.

## Question 7

Besides absorbing CO2, what else do forests do for our planet?

- [x] Provide homes for millions of animal and plant species
  > Absolutely right! Forests are like nature's apartment buildings - they provide food, shelter, and homes for countless species, from tiny insects to large mammals.

- [ ] Nothing else - they only absorb CO2
  > Nice try! Forests are actually multitaskers. They do so much more than just absorb CO2 - they're home to millions of species!

- [ ] They only exist for humans to use for wood
  > Nice try! While we do use wood, forests have a much bigger purpose - they support entire ecosystems of plants and animals.

- [ ] They block all sunlight from reaching the ground
  > Nice try! While forests do provide shade, some sunlight still reaches the forest floor, and forests have many more important roles.

> Hint: Think about all the animals that live in forests!

## Question 8

How do forests help keep the Earth cool?

- [ ] They blow cold air with their leaves
  > Nice try! Leaves don't create cold air. Forests help cool the Earth in a much more scientific way - by removing heat-trapping gases.

- [x] They remove carbon dioxide, which traps heat in the atmosphere
  > Excellent understanding! By absorbing CO2 (a greenhouse gas), forests reduce the amount of heat trapped in our atmosphere. Less CO2 = less warming!

- [ ] They freeze the water in clouds
  > Nice try! Forests don't freeze clouds. They help cool Earth by absorbing carbon dioxide, a gas that would otherwise trap heat.

- [ ] They paint the sky blue
  > Nice try! That's not how it works. Forests help cool Earth by absorbing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.

> Hint: Think about what gas forests absorb and what that gas does to temperatures.

## Question 9

Which type of forest absorbs the MOST carbon dioxide?

- [ ] Young, newly planted forests
  > Nice try! Young forests are growing and helpful, but they're still small. Older, larger forests with big trees absorb more CO2.

- [x] Old-growth forests with large, mature trees
  > You got it! Old-growth forests with big, mature trees are carbon-absorbing champions. Their huge trunks, branches, and extensive root systems store massive amounts of carbon.

- [ ] Forests with only one type of tree
  > Nice try! Forests with diverse trees are actually healthier. But the key to absorbing more CO2 is having large, mature trees.

- [ ] Dead forests with fallen trees
  > Nice try! Dead trees actually release carbon as they decompose. Living, healthy forests with big trees absorb the most CO2.

> Hint: Think about tree size - bigger trees have more space to store carbon!

## Question 10

What can YOU do to help protect forests?

- [x] Use less paper and recycle what you use
  > Wonderful! Using less paper means fewer trees need to be cut down. And recycling paper means those trees didn't give up their carbon for nothing. You're a forest hero!

- [ ] Use as much paper as possible
  > Nice try! Using lots of paper actually means more trees need to be cut down. We should try to use less paper and always recycle.

- [ ] There's nothing kids can do to help forests
  > Nice try! Kids can absolutely help! You can recycle paper, plant trees, and spread the word about why forests matter.

- [ ] Burn leaves in your backyard
  > Nice try! Burning anything releases carbon into the air, which is the opposite of what we want. Composting leaves is much better!

> Hint: Paper comes from trees. What could you do to help save trees?
