## Question 1

Which form of carbon is found mainly in Earth's atmosphere?

- [ ] Methane
  > Nice try! Methane is in the air, but only in a very tiny amount, so it's not the main form of carbon we find in the atmosphere.

- [ ] Bicarbonate
  > Nice try! Bicarbonate is usually found in water, like oceans and inside living things, not in the air we breathe.

- [ ] Carbonic acid
  > Nice try! Carbonic acid only forms when carbon mixes with water, so it can't mainly stay in the atmosphere.

- [x] Carbon dioxide
  > You got it right! Carbon dioxide is the main form of carbon gas present in Earth's atmosphere.

> Hint: Think about what gas plants absorb and what we breathe out!

## Question 2

Why is carbon dioxide described as a greenhouse gas?

- [ ] It helps plants grow taller.
  > Nice try! Plants use carbon-containing gases for growth, but this is not the reason it is called a greenhouse gas.

- [x] It traps heat in the atmosphere.
  > Well done! Like a greenhouse, this gas holds heat in the air, helping warm the Earth.

- [ ] It provides oxygen for animals.
  > Nice try! Animals need oxygen to breathe, but this gas does not supply oxygen to living things.

- [ ] It breaks down food in animals.
  > Nice try! Food breakdown happens inside the body through digestion, not because of gases in the atmosphere.

> Hint: Think about what a greenhouse does - it keeps things warm inside!

## Question 3

A tree in the school yard is exposed to sunlight and carbon dioxide. What process is most likely happening inside its leaves?

- [ ] Respiration
  > Nice try! Respiration happens in living things, but this process releases energy - it does not use sunlight to make food.

- [ ] Combustion
  > Nice try! Combustion involves burning and heat, which does not occur inside plant leaves.

- [x] Photosynthesis
  > That's correct! Leaves use sunlight and carbon dioxide to make food for the plant.

- [ ] Decomposition
  > Nice try! Decomposition happens when organisms break down dead matter, not in a living tree.

> Hint: This process uses light (photo) to put together (synthesize) food!

## Question 4

Which sequence best shows how carbon moves through the food web?

- [ ] Soil to Sun to Animal to Air
  > Nice try! Carbon can be found in the soil, but it does not move from the soil to the Sun. The Sun gives energy, not carbon.

- [ ] Air to Animal to Plant to Soil
  > Nice try! Animals do not get carbon directly from the air.

- [ ] Animal to Sun to Plant to Air
  > Nice try! Carbon does not travel to or from the Sun. It moves only among living things and the environment.

- [x] Plant to Animal to Other animals
  > Well done! Carbon moves as organisms eat one another, passing carbon through the food web.

> Hint: Think about who eats whom in nature!

## Question 5

Which statement best explains why carbon connects plants, animals, and humans?

- [ ] Carbon is mainly used by plants to produce oxygen.
  > Nice try! Plants do release oxygen, but carbon is mainly used to make food for the plant.

- [x] Carbon moves through living things as food and energy.
  > Very Good! Carbon passes from plants to animals and humans through food, connecting all living organisms.

- [ ] Carbon cycles between the air and soil in natural environments.
  > Nice try! Carbon does move between air and soil, but this does not fully explain how living things are connected.

- [ ] Carbon remains in the atmosphere as a greenhouse gas that helps regulate heat.
  > Nice try! Carbon can stay in the air, but this does not show how plants, animals, and humans are linked.

> Hint: Think about what happens when you eat food - where does that energy come from?

## Question 6

What role do decomposers play in the carbon cycle?

- [ ] They create new carbon atoms from nothing.
  > Nice try! Carbon atoms cannot be created or destroyed - they can only move around. Decomposers help recycle carbon that already exists!

- [x] They break down dead organisms and release carbon back into the environment.
  > Excellent! Decomposers like bacteria and fungi break down dead plants and animals, releasing the carbon stored in them back into the soil and air.

- [ ] They absorb carbon dioxide directly from the sun.
  > Nice try! The sun provides energy, not carbon dioxide. Decomposers work on dead matter, not sunlight!

- [ ] They store carbon permanently in rocks.
  > Nice try! While some carbon does end up in rocks over millions of years, decomposers mainly release carbon back into the air and soil.

> Hint: Think about what happens to fallen leaves and dead animals in nature!

## Question 7

How does carbon move from the ocean to the atmosphere?

- [ ] Fish carry it up to the surface.
  > Nice try! Fish don't carry carbon to the atmosphere. Carbon moves between ocean and air through natural processes at the water's surface.

- [x] Carbon dioxide gas is released from the ocean surface into the air.
  > That's right! The ocean and atmosphere constantly exchange carbon dioxide at the surface. When ocean water warms up, it releases more CO2 into the air.

- [ ] Waves throw carbon into the sky.
  > Nice try! Waves don't throw carbon into the sky. Carbon dioxide gas naturally moves between the ocean surface and the atmosphere.

- [ ] Boats transport carbon from water to land.
  > Nice try! Boats don't transport carbon this way. Carbon moves naturally as gas between the ocean and atmosphere.

> Hint: Think about gases escaping from water, like bubbles in a soda!

## Question 8

What would happen if all the plants on Earth suddenly disappeared?

- [ ] Carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere would decrease.
  > Nice try! Without plants to absorb CO2, the levels would actually increase, not decrease!

- [x] Carbon dioxide would build up in the atmosphere because nothing would absorb it.
  > Correct! Plants are essential for removing CO2 from the air. Without them, carbon dioxide would keep building up, making Earth much warmer.

- [ ] Animals would start doing photosynthesis instead.
  > Nice try! Animals cannot do photosynthesis - only plants and some bacteria can. We depend on plants for this important job!

- [ ] Nothing would change because carbon doesn't need plants.
  > Nice try! Plants are crucial to the carbon cycle. Without them, the whole system would be thrown out of balance.

> Hint: Remember what plants do with carbon dioxide!

## Question 9

Which part of the carbon cycle takes the longest time?

- [ ] A plant absorbing CO2 for photosynthesis
  > Nice try! Photosynthesis happens quickly, every day when the sun shines. Some carbon processes take much, much longer!

- [ ] An animal breathing out carbon dioxide
  > Nice try! Breathing happens in seconds. Some carbon stays locked away for millions of years!

- [x] Carbon being stored deep underground as fossil fuels
  > Excellent thinking! Carbon can be stored underground for millions of years before becoming fossil fuels. This is the slowest part of the carbon cycle!

- [ ] Decomposers breaking down a fallen leaf
  > Nice try! Decomposition takes weeks or months. Forming fossil fuels takes millions of years - that's the slowest part!

> Hint: Think about how long it takes for fossil fuels to form!

## Question 10

Why is understanding the carbon cycle important for protecting our planet?

- [ ] It helps us know when to water our gardens.
  > Nice try! Watering gardens is about water, not carbon. Understanding the carbon cycle helps us fight climate change!

- [x] It shows us how our actions affect the balance of carbon and climate.
  > Wonderful! When we understand how carbon moves through nature, we can make better choices to keep the cycle balanced and protect our planet from climate change.

- [ ] It tells us which foods have the most vitamins.
  > Nice try! Nutrition is different from the carbon cycle. Understanding carbon helps us understand climate change!

- [ ] It has nothing to do with protecting the planet.
  > Nice try! The carbon cycle is directly connected to climate change. Understanding it helps us make choices that protect Earth!

> Hint: Think about how human activities can change the amount of carbon in the air!
